## B. To retrieve yourself

### Theme 1 — Fractional volume (indirect = ½ set) & volume quantification

1. **Baz-Valle E, Fontes-Villalba M, Santos-Concejero J (2021).** *Total Number of Sets as a
   Training Volume Quantification Method for Muscle Hypertrophy: A Systematic Review.* J Strength
   Cond Res 35(3):870–878. DOI 10.1519/JSC.0000000000002776 — **✓ RETRIEVED — now in Bucket A**
   (`Baz-Valle-2021_total-sets-volume-quantification.pdf`).
   *Why:* The methodological case for counting "sets" (not volume-load) and what counts as a set.

2. **Baz-Valle E, Balsalobre-Fernández C, Alix-Fages C, Santos-Concejero J (2022).** *A
   Systematic Review of the Effects of Different Resistance Training Volumes on Muscle
   Hypertrophy.* J Hum Kinet 81:199–210. DOI 10.2478/hukin-2022-0017 — **✓ RETRIEVED — now in
   Bucket A** (`Baz-Valle-2022_volume-effects-hypertrophy-review.pdf`).
   *Why:* Volume effects synthesis incl. the >20 sets/wk plateau; pairs with theme 4.

3. **Renaissance Periodization — Israetel M, Hoffmann J, Smith CW.** *Scientific Principles of
   Hypertrophy Training* (ebook) + RP volume-landmark articles (MV / MEV / MAV / MRV).
   *Why:* Origin of mainstream per-muscle fractional/indirect set bookkeeping; bridges to theme 3.
   **✓ Data captured — now in Bucket A** as a fresh scrape of the live RP pages

   already captured in the CSV; the book is just paid rationale.

### Theme 2 — Which exercises are fractional for which muscles (direct/indirect mapping + EMG)

4. **EMG / %MVC of synergist ("indirect") movers in the major compound lifts.** Empirical
   activation data used to justify which muscles a compound trains indirectly (the 0.5 weighting),
   organized by lift. All citations below are **verified** (Crossref/PMC). Read alongside the
   **Vigotsky 2018 caveat (Bucket A)**: EMG amplitude is a proxy for activation, **not** a direct
   measure of hypertrophic stimulus — use these to rank/triage, not as ground truth.

   - **Back (pulldown / row) — lats + biceps synergist:** **Lehman GJ, Buchan DD, Lundy A, Myers N,
     Nalborczyk A (2004).** *Variations in muscle activation levels during traditional latissimus
     dorsi weight training exercises.* Dynamic Medicine 3:4. DOI 10.1186/1476-5918-3-4 (PMID
     15228624 / PMC449729). — **✓ RETRIEVED — Bucket A**
     (`Lehman-2004_emg-latissimus-biceps-pulldown-row-variations.pdf`). *Open access.*
   - **Bench press — pec + anterior deltoid + triceps synergist:** **Rodríguez-Ridao D,
     Antequera-Vique JA, Martín-Fuentes I, Muyor JM (2020).** *Effect of Five Bench Inclinations on
     the Electromyographic Activity of the Pectoralis Major, Anterior Deltoid, and Triceps Brachii
     during the Bench Press Exercise.* Int J Environ Res Public Health 17(19):7339. DOI
     10.3390/ijerph17197339 (PMC7579505). — **✓ RETRIEVED — Bucket A**
     (`Rodriguez-Ridao-2020_emg-bench-press-inclinations-pec-delt-triceps.pdf`). *Open access.*
   - **Deadlift — posterior chain (erectors, glutes, hamstrings) + quads:** **Martín-Fuentes I,
     Oliva-Lozano JM, Muyor JM (2020).** *Electromyographic activity in deadlift exercise and its
     variants. A systematic review.* PLoS ONE 15(2):e0229507. DOI 10.1371/journal.pone.0229507. —
     **✓ RETRIEVED — Bucket A** (`Martin-Fuentes-2020_emg-deadlift-variants-systematic-review.pdf`).
     *Open access; aggregates %MVC across studies — the single best per-muscle table for the DL.*
   - **Squat — glutes, hamstrings, adductors, quads:** **Clark DR, Lambert MI, Hunter AM (2012).**
     *Muscle activation in the loaded free barbell squat: a brief review.* J Strength Cond Res
     26(4):1169–1178. DOI 10.1519/JSC.0b013e31822d533d. — **✓ RETRIEVED — Bucket A**
     (`Clark-2012_emg-loaded-barbell-squat-review.pdf`). Review of which muscles the squat
     activates and at what %MVC.
   - **Bench press (second source) — pec/deltoid/triceps:** **Saeterbakken AH, Fimland MS (2013).**
     *Electromyographic activity and 6RM strength in bench press on stable and unstable surfaces.*
     J Strength Cond Res 27(4):1101–1107. DOI 10.1519/JSC.0b013e3182606d3d. — **✓ RETRIEVED —
     Bucket A** (`Saeterbakken-2013_emg-6rm-bench-press-stable-unstable.pdf`). ⚠️ *Reports
     **absolute RMS mV**, NOT %MVC — the authors deliberately did not normalize — and the values
     live only in bar-chart figures (Figs 2–5). Useful for the relative pec/delt/triceps activation
     ordering in the bench press, but not directly comparable to the %MVC papers above; would need
     figure-digitizing for numbers.*

5. **Vigotsky AD, Halperin I, Lehman GJ, Trajano GS, Vieira TM (2018).** *Interpreting Signal
   Amplitudes in Surface Electromyography Studies in Sport and Rehabilitation Sciences.* Front
   Physiol 8:985. DOI 10.3389/fphys.2017.00985 — **✓ RETRIEVED — now in Bucket A**
   (`Vigotsky-2018_interpreting-emg-signal-amplitudes.pdf`).
   *Why:* Critical caveat: EMG amplitude ≠ hypertrophic stimulus. Keeps the mapping honest.

6. **Free Exercise DB (yuhonas/free-exercise-db).** Public-domain dataset, 873 exercises, each with
   primary (direct) / secondary (indirect) muscles, mechanic (compound/isolation), force, equipment
   and category. — **✓ VENDORED — now in Bucket A** (`free-exercise-db_exercises.json` +
   `free-exercise-db_mover-map.csv`, provenance/license in `free-exercise-db_SOURCES.md`).
   *Why:* The broad exercise→muscle mover map across ALL exercises (not just the big compounds the
   EMG papers cover), and the master exercise list for later aggregation. `primaryMuscles` → full
   set, `secondaryMuscles` → 0.5 set — exactly the direct/indirect split. **Replaces the print
   textbooks** for this purpose (see #6b). Community-curated, not EMG-derived — cross-check the big
   lifts against the EMG papers above.

6b. **Print textbooks (NSCA *Essentials of S&C* 4th ed.; Delavier *Strength Training Anatomy* 3rd
    ed.; Zatsiorsky & Kraemer *Science and Practice of Strength Training* 3rd ed.).** — **🚫 DECLINING
    TO COLLECT.** Their load-bearing content is already covered: mover mapping → Free Exercise DB
    (#6) + the EMG %MVC papers (#4); SRA/supercompensation & training-timing → Clarke & Skiba 2013
    (#9) + Busso 2003 (#8) + the short-term recovery papers (#11/#12, Paulsen). The books would be

### Theme 3 — Math of recovery per muscle

**3a. Longer-term performance/recovery models (parameterized by load/effort):**

7. **Banister EW, Calvert TW, Savage MV, Bach T (1975).** *A systems model of training for
   athletic performance.* Aust J Sports Med 7:57–61. — **🚫 DECLINING TO COLLECT.** Content fully
   subsumed by #9 (Clarke & Skiba) and #8 (Busso), both retrieved; archival and hard to source;
   its parameter values are not canonical anyway. Cite secondhand if needed.
   *Why (for context):* The original fitness–fatigue (impulse–response) model — performance =
   baseline + fitness − fatigue, two exponentials — still the standard structure. **Its parameter
   values are not canonical:** the time constants (~τ_fitness 45 d / τ_fatigue 15 d) were fit to
   *endurance* athletes via TRIMP, not resistance training and not per-muscle. See #7b for the
   identifiability caveat and #9 for an accessible re-derivation + fitting guide.

7b. **Hellard P, Avalos M, Lacoste L, Barale F, Chatard JC, Millet GP (2006).** *Assessing the
   limitations of the Banister model in monitoring training.* Int J Sports Med 27(1):62–68. DOI
   10.1055/s-2005-837507 — **Paywalled.** PMID 16320189.
   *Why:* Shows the model is ill-conditioned / poorly identifiable — many parameter sets fit the
   same data and τ_fatigue is unstable. Essential context so Banister's numbers aren't
   over-interpreted. This is *refinement*, not disagreement with the framework.

8. **Busso T (2003).** *Variable dose-response relationship between exercise training and
   performance.* Med Sci Sports Exerc 35(7):1188–1195. DOI 10.1249/01.MSS.0000074465.13621.37
   — **✓ RETRIEVED — now in Bucket A**
   (`Busso-2003_variable-dose-response-training-performance.pdf`). PMID 12840641.
   *Why:* Time-varying-gain extension of Banister; explicitly dose→fatigue/recovery dynamics.

9. **Clarke DC, Skiba PF (2013).** *Rationale and resources for teaching the mathematical
   modeling of athletic training and performance.* Adv Physiol Educ 37(2):134–152. DOI
   10.1152/advan.00078.2011 — **✓ RETRIEVED — now in Bucket A**
   (`Clarke-2013_fitness-fatigue-modeling-tutorial.pdf` + `..._corrigendum.pdf`).
   *Why:* The most accessible, equation-by-equation tutorial on fitness–fatigue / impulse-response
   models, with worked examples. Best entry point for the math. **High priority.**

10. **Zatsiorsky VM, Kraemer WJ, Fry AC. *Science and Practice of Strength Training* (3rd ed.,
    2020), chapters on timing of training & the SRA/supercompensation curve.** — **🚫 DECLINING TO
    COLLECT** (see #6b). The SRA/training-timing content is covered by the fitness-fatigue math in
    #9 (Clarke & Skiba) + #8 (Busso) and the short-term recovery papers (#11/#12).

**3b. Short-term physiological recovery (within/between sessions):**

11. **Damas F, Phillips S, Vechin FC, Ugrinowitsch C (2015).** *A review of resistance
    training-induced changes in skeletal muscle protein synthesis and their contribution to
    hypertrophy.* Sports Med 45(6):801–807. DOI 10.1007/s40279-015-0320-0 — **✓ RETRIEVED — now
    in Bucket A** (`Damas-2015_rt-muscle-protein-synthesis-hypertrophy-review.pdf`).
    *Why:* MPS time-course (24–72 h) and how the damage component shrinks with training status.

12. **Howatson G, van Someren KA (2008).** *The prevention and treatment of exercise-induced
    muscle damage.* Sports Med 38(6):483–503. DOI 10.2165/00007256-200838060-00004 — **✓ RETRIEVED
    — now in Bucket A** (`Howatson-2008_prevention-treatment-exercise-induced-muscle-damage.pdf`).
    *Why:* EIMD time courses, repeated-bout effect, per-protocol recovery.

13. **Schoenfeld BJ, Grgic J, Krieger J (2019).** *How many times per week should a muscle be
    trained to maximize muscle hypertrophy? A systematic review and meta-analysis of studies
    examining the effects of resistance training frequency.* J Sports Sci 37(11):1286–1295. DOI
    10.1080/02640414.2018.1555906 — **✓ RETRIEVED — now in Bucket A**
    (`Schoenfeld-2019_training-frequency-hypertrophy-meta-analysis.pdf`).
    *Why:* Ties recovery dynamics to applied training frequency (volume-equated).
